Introduction

Genetic Association Studies

Goal: detect the association of genetic variants with a particular trait of interest, e.g. disease or quantitative trait

Analysis strategies

Several statistical analysis strategies proposed (Balding 2006):

  • binary trait
    • Pearson's \(\chi^{2}\)-square test for independence testing
    • the Cochran-Armitage trend test (Armitage 1955)
    • multiple two-by-two tables (Cheng and Lee 2014)
    • the 2 degrees of freedom likelihood ratio test based on logistic regression (Wellek and Ziegler 2012)
  • quantitative traits - linear regression and the corresponding F-test
  • count phenotypes - Poisson regression approach (Qu 2014)
  • survival outcomes - tests based on the Cox proportional hazards model (Mendolia et al. 2014)

The genetic mode on inheritance

Commonly thee genetic modes of inheritance are considered (Freidlin et al. 2002):

  • dominant model - assumes that either one or two copies of the minor allele are sufficient to produce an (equal) effect on the trait
  • additive model - assumes that the trait effect is linearly related to the number of minor alleles
  • recessive model - assumes that the trait effect is related to the presence of both minor alleles

In reality the mode of inheritance is rarely known a-priori in genetic association studies.

Methods

Methods

  • consider an autosomal diallelic marker (e.g., SNP) with alleles \(A\) and \(a\).
  • genotypes (\(AA\), \(Aa\) and \(Aa\)) are represented by numeric variable \(x_{i}\), with \(i = 1,\ldots,n\) independent subjects
  • \(x_{i}\) corresponds to the number of copies of the allele \(a\), i.e., if subject \(i\) has zero copies of the allele \(a\) then \(x_{i}=0\), and so on
  • consider the generalized linear model (Nelder and Wedderburn 1972): \[ E(y_{i})=g^{-1}\left(x_{i} \beta + \mathbf{x}_{Ni}'\mathbf{\beta}_N\right) \] , with e.g. logit link, identity link, or proportional hazards model

Multiple genetic models

Assess the effect of genotype on the trait under different modes of inheritance consider the following scores:

genetic model independent variable AA Aa aa
dominant \(x^{(1)}\) 0 1 1
additive \(x^{(2)}\) 0 0.5 1
recessive \(x^{(3)}\) 0 0 1

with the vector of all parameters \[\mathbf{\beta}^{j} = (\beta^{j}, \mathbf{\beta}_{N})\] with \(j = 1,2,3\)

Testing marker effects

To test the genetic marker for its association with the phenotype of interest it is assumed that we are interested in the following null hypothesis

\[H_{0}^{(j)}: \hspace{0.5cm} \beta^{(j)}=\beta^{(j)}_{0}, \qquad j=1,2,3,\] Use the multiple marginal models approach of Pipper, Ritz, and Bisgaard (2012) (see also previous talks) to consider simultaneous inference on the parameter vectors from the three genetic models \(\mathbf{\beta^{(123)}}=(\mathbf{\beta^{(1)}}, \mathbf{\beta^{(2)}},\mathbf{\beta^{(3)}})\) (see also (So and Sham 2011, Kitsche, Ritz, and Hothorn (2016))).

Conclusions

  • The proposed approach covers a wide variety of endpoints (binary, count, quantitative, and time-to-event data)
  • Multiple endpoints of different types can be assessed symultaneously (analysis of pleiotropic effects)
  • multiple loci can be assessed simultaneously while taking different modes of inheritance into account
